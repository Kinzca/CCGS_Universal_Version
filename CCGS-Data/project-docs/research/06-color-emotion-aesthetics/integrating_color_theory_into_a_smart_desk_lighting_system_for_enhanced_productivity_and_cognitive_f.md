 Abstract 

In the age of remote and hybrid work, the quality of the personal workspace has a significant impact on cognitive performance, productivity, and overall well-being. Among the most influential yet often overlooked environmental factors is lighting. While studies have shown that lighting affects focus, mood, circadian rhythms, and mental clarity, most commercially available workspace lighting solutions remain static, one-size-fits-all, and disconnected from user needs. 

This thesis investigates how lighting can be intentionally designed to support focus, reduce stress, and enhance work experience through a human-centered design approach. Drawing from interdisciplinary research in lighting ergonomics, color theory, environmental psychology, and UX/UI design, the study explores how adaptable lighting conditions can influence user behavior, cognitive function, and workspace satisfaction. 

The project follows a comprehensive research process involving user surveys, environmental analysis, and performance-based testing to uncover lighting challenges and preferences in real-world desk environments. These findings will inform the design and development of a smart desk lighting system tailored to the needs of modern workers. The final product will integrate dynamic control, customizable lighting profiles, and aesthetic versatility to create a system that aligns with both functional and emotional workspace demands. 

This thesis contributes to the field of industrial design by demonstrating how research-driven, human-centered solutions can elevate everyday products and foster healthier, more productive environments in an evolving world of work. 

Keywords: Smart lighting, human-centered design, workspace ergonomics, cognitive performance, color theory, UX/UI integration

Chapter I: Introduction 

1.1 Background 

In recent years, the shift toward remote and hybrid work models has brought increased attention to the quality of home and personal workspaces. As more people engage in desk-based tasks for extended periods, the impact of environmental factors—particularly lighting—on productivity, mental well-being, and cognitive performance has become more apparent. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics (2021), professionals spend over six hours a day at their desks, often longer in remote roles. Despite this, workspace lighting remains one of the most overlooked contributors to physical comfort and mental focus. 

Research has shown that lighting conditions can significantly influence cognitive function, emotional state, and physical health. Poor lighting—such as excessive glare or low brightness—can lead to eye strain, fatigue, and decreased concentration (IES, 2021). Conversely, proper lighting supports visual comfort, alertness, and mood regulation (Smolders & de Kort, 2014). Moreover, exposure to different light color temperatures and intensities can directly affect circadian rhythms, influencing sleep quality and daily energy levels. 

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic further emphasized the need for more adaptable and intentional workspace environments. As kitchen tables and spare bedrooms became makeshift offices, individuals began seeking ways to improve the quality of their workspaces to support long hours of screen time, creative thinking, and focused productivity. Lighting in particular emerged as a crucial factor in maintaining concentration and well-being throughout the day. Yet, most commercially available lighting systems remain static, one-dimensional, and disconnected from users' behavioral and psychological needs. 

This research is rooted in the growing recognition that well-designed lighting systems can significantly improve how we feel and function at work. It explores how lighting—when approached through the lens of human-centered design—can become a tool not only for visibility, but for enhancing cognitive performance and emotional balance in the workspace. 

1.2 Project Description 

This thesis explores how a human-centered approach to lighting design can improve cognitive performance, productivity, and well-being in modern workspaces. The project investigates the intersection of lighting ergonomics, color theory, behavioral science, and workspace design to better understand how lighting conditions affect users’ mental clarity, focus, and comfort. 

The research is grounded in the belief that lighting should be responsive, adaptable, and supportive of various tasks, time-of-day needs, and personal preferences—especially in the context of flexible and home-based work environments. Rather than beginning with a predetermined solution, this study adopts an exploratory approach, using qualitative and quantitative methods to uncover user behaviors, environmental challenges, and opportunities for meaningful design intervention. 

2 

The project will follow a human-centered design process that includes literature review, user surveys, environmental analysis, and cognitive performance testing. Through these methods, the researcher will identify user needs, define design criteria, and evaluate how lighting variables—such as color temperature, brightness, and adjustability—can be optimized to support different modes of work and mental states. 

Following the research and testing phases, the insights gathered will inform the design and development of a product: a smart desk lighting system tailored to support focus, reduce cognitive fatigue, and enhance the emotional quality of the workspace. This final design will synthesize the research findings into a physical solution, demonstrating how thoughtful lighting design can bridge functionality, well-being, and user experience in the evolving landscape of work. 

1.3 Research Problem and Study Significance 

While the relationship between lighting and human performance is well-documented, current workspace lighting products often fail to address the needs of individuals in real-world settings. Many existing systems prioritize basic illumination without considering factors like task-specific light behavior, user preference, environmental context, or adaptability. As a result, users frequently experience discomfort, distraction, or inefficiency in their workspaces. 

This research addresses the need for lighting systems that align with cognitive science, behavioral needs, and modern workspace habits. The significance of this study lies in its potential to bridge the gap between technical lighting capability and user-centered experience—resulting in designs that enhance not only visibility, but also performance, mood, and overall quality of life. 

As flexible, home-based, and hybrid work continues to grow, the demand for intentional workspace tools will only increase. By investigating the potential of adaptive lighting systems, this study contributes to broader conversations in industrial design, environmental psychology, and wellness-centered innovation.  

By blending technical research with user-driven insights, this project ultimately seeks to answer the question: How can we design workspace lighting that actively supports cognitive performance, emotional well-being, and task efficiency in a diverse range of environments?

The primary objective of this research is to explore how dynamic, user-centered lighting systems can improve cognitive and emotional performance in workspace environments. The specific research objectives are as follows: 

1. To examine the psychological and physiological effects of different lighting conditions on focus, productivity, and well-being. 

2. To identify user needs, preferences, and pain points related to lighting in personal and professional workspaces. 

3. To explore how principles of color theory, light ergonomics, and circadian lighting can inform the development of responsive lighting systems.

1.5 Conceptual Framework 

The conceptual framework for this thesis is grounded in Human-Centered Design (HCD), supported by research in lighting ergonomics, color psychology, environmental behavior, and UX/UI interaction. The framework emphasizes the relationship between users, their environment, and the tools they use— positioning lighting as both a functional and emotional mediator in workspace performance. 

Key elements of the framework include: 

User Behavior & Workspace Interaction: Understanding how users engage with their workspace lighting during different types of tasks and times of day. 

Lighting & Cognitive Function: Investigating how light intensity, temperature, and color affect attention, memory retention, and emotional state. 

Personalization & Control: Exploring how the ability to adjust lighting can improve the user experience and sense of agency in the workspace. 

System Integration: Considering how digital and physical components (e.g., lighting hardware and control interface) work together seamlessly. 

Aesthetic and Spatial Consideration: Designing lighting systems that integrate visually and functionally into modern home and office spaces. 

This framework will guide the research, design exploration, and eventual development of a lighting solution that reflects the complex interplay between the human mind, body, and environment. It sets the stage for an iterative and reflective design process—one that follows the researcher’s journey from investigation to insight, and ultimately toward an innovative outcome grounded in empathy and evidence.

Chapter II: Literature Review 

2.1 Historical Context and Evolution 

Lighting has always played a foundational role in how humans live, work, and interact with their environments. Before artificial light, daily activities were dictated by the sun, with natural light serving as both a source of visibility and a regulator of biological rhythms, including the sleep-wake cycle. 

The development of firelights, oil lamps, and gas lighting marked early advances in artificial illumination. However, the invention of the electric incandescent bulb by Thomas Edison in the late 19th century revolutionized modern life, extending productivity and social activity into nighttime hours (See figure 2.1). These early systems were largely uniform in color and intensity, focusing on functional illumination without consideration for psychological or physiological effects. 

The 20th century brought rapid technological progress with fluorescent and halogen lighting, which became standard in homes, workplaces, and institutions. However, these technologies offered limited control over light quality. The recent emergence of LED (Light-Emitting Diode) technology introduced adaptable, efficient, and customizable lighting options, capable of being fine-tuned to user preferences and environmental needs (See figure 2.2). 

Alongside technological development, research into light's non-visual effects—such as its impact on mood, alertness, and circadian rhythms—has grown. Lighting is now seen as a crucial element of environmental ergonomics, with contemporary design increasingly focused on human-centered and health-conscious approaches (Aries, 2005; Wurtman, 1975; Figueiro et al., 2004).

2.2 Lighting Terminology 

To effectively understand the role of lighting in workspace design and its impact on cognitive function, it is important to establish key terminology commonly used in lighting science, design, and user experience. The following terms provide a foundation for interpreting research findings, evaluating design decisions, and understanding user-centered lighting systems. 

Illuminance (Lux): The amount of light reaching a surface, measured in lux (lx). Office work typically requires 300–500 lux; detailed tasks may need up to 1000 lux (See figure 2.3). 

6 

Figure 2.3: Lumen vs Lux vs Candela. 

Luminance: The perceived brightness of a surface, influenced by light intensity and surface reflectivity. High contrast levels can cause visual discomfort (See figure 2.4). 

Figure 2.4: Terminology to describe various measures of light includes luminance, luminous intensity, luminous flux, and illuminance (CK Associates, 2020). 

Color Temperature (Kelvin): The hue of white light, measured in Kelvin (K). Warm light (2700–3000K) appears yellow-orange; cool light (5000–6500K) appears bluish-white (See figure 2.5). 

Correlated Color Temperature (CCT): A more precise descriptor of white light appearance, crucial in mood regulation and circadian-supportive lighting. 

Color Rendering Index (CRI): A 0–100 scale measuring how accurately a light source renders colors compared to natural sunlight. A CRI of 80+ is recommended for most environments. 

Circadian Lighting: Lighting systems that shift color temperature and intensity throughout the day to align with the body's natural rhythms (See figure 2.5). 

7 

Figure 2.5: Kelvin Color Temperature Scale Throughout the Day. 

Task Lighting: Adjustable lighting focused on work areas to reduce glare and support high-focus activities. 

Ambient Lighting: General, diffused lighting that establishes base visibility and comfort. 

Direct Lighting: Lighting is directed precisely toward a surface or object, creating high illumination levels in a specific area. Often used for tasks that require focused attention, such as reading or drafting, direct lighting can increase visibility but must be managed to avoid harsh shadows and glare. 

Glare: Excessively bright light that reduces visibility or causes discomfort. Controlled using diffusers, strategic placement, or shading. 

RGB Lighting: Red, green, and blue LED systems that create a wide range of colors, often used to influence mood or personalize environments (See figures 2.6 and 2.7).

2.3 Color Theory and Its Impact on Cognitive Function 

Color theory, rooted in artistic and scientific traditions, explores how hue, saturation, and brightness influence perception and behavior. In lighting design, color temperature plays a significant role, ranging from warm tones (2700–3000K) that create a cozy ambiance to cool tones (5000–6500K) that enhance focus and alertness. 

Studies show that colors influence emotional and cognitive states. Blue hues are linked with calmness and mental clarity, while reds and oranges can boost energy but may also induce stress in high-focus settings (Elliot & Maier, 2007; Valdez & Mehrabian, 1994; D'Andrade & Egan, 1974). Al-Ayash et al. (2015) found that environmental color impacts heart rate and performance in learning environments. 

In workspace lighting, color can be strategically applied to suit various tasks (See figure 2.8). Cool light supports sustained focus, while warmer tones may enhance creativity or relaxation. Dynamic lighting systems offering user control over color and temperature reduce monotony and improve engagement (Schatz & Bowers, 2005; Kareklas et al., 2014).

2.4 Lighting Design with Focus on Workspace Lighting 

Lighting significantly influences both the physical and cognitive aspects of workspace environments. With remote and hybrid work on the rise, the importance of lighting systems that support both clarity and well-being has increased. 

Natural daylight remains the preferred lighting source due to its alignment with circadian rhythms and high color rendering (See figure 2.9). Exposure to daylight improves mood, sleep quality, and productivity (Viola et al., 2008; Aries, 2005). However, architectural constraints often limit natural light, requiring effective artificial supplementation. 

Figure 2.9: Daylight mimicking artificial light. 

Artificial lighting, particularly adjustable white light, allows for simulated daylight cycles. Cool white light (5000–6500K) promotes concentration, while warm light (2700–3000K) encourages relaxation. Tunable systems help maintain comfort and circadian health (Bao et al., 2021). 

RGB lighting adds aesthetic and psychological flexibility, offering dynamic control over color environments. Once associated with leisure, RGB systems are now being incorporated into professional settings to support mood and mental engagement. When thoughtfully applied, they contribute to satisfaction and improved cognitive clarity. 

A well-designed lighting system combines natural and artificial sources to provide flexible, responsive illumination (See figure 2.10). In home offices, where lighting conditions vary, adaptability in both intensity and color is essential to comfort and performance.

2.5 Effects of Light on Productivity and Cognitive Function 

Lighting directly influences how individuals feel, think, and perform. In workspaces—especially those requiring sustained concentration or long periods of screen use—its role extends beyond visibility, impacting psychological and physiological states. 

Poor lighting conditions, including low illuminance, poor color rendering, or glare, contribute to fatigue, reduced motivation, and elevated stress. In contrast, effective lighting enhances mood, focus, and task performance (Gupta, 2021; Amorim et al., 2022). 

Cool white light (5000–6500K) is associated with greater alertness and improved accuracy, particularly during cognitively demanding tasks. Studies by Smolders and de Kort (2014) and Bao et al. (2021) demonstrate the cognitive benefits of high color temperature lighting. In contrast, warmer light may reduce mental sharpness but supports relaxation and creativity. 

Lighting intensity also plays a critical role in mental stamina. Bright, cool light during the day enhances physiological arousal and reduces mental fatigue (Chellappa et al., 2011). This is particularly valuable during early morning or post-lunch periods when natural energy dips. 

Visual comfort further influences productivity. Inadequate or overly intense lighting can lead to eye strain and distraction. Glare from screens or reflective surfaces forces visual overcompensation, leading to discomfort and cognitive fatigue (Han et al., 2021; Osterhaus et al., 2015). Well-balanced lighting should offer even distribution, minimal flicker, and appropriate contrast. 

11 

Lighting systems that support task performance should be adjustable, user-responsive, and considerate of spatial needs. As home work environments vary widely, the ability to tailor lighting to individual tasks and preferences is increasingly important to promoting focus and well-being. 

2.6 Human Factor Guidelines and Considerations 

Human factors are essential in lighting design, especially in contexts where mental load, comfort, and long-term health are priorities (See figure 2.11). A human-centered approach considers ergonomics, cognitive processing, emotional response, and intuitive interaction between users and lighting systems (McKee & Hedge, 2022). 

Figure 2.11: Ergonomic requirements in an office. 

A key biological factor in lighting design is the circadian rhythm—the body’s internal 24-hour clock regulating sleep, hormone cycles, and alertness (See figure 2.12). Light is its primary synchronizer, or zeitgeber. Morning exposure to blue-enriched light supports alertness and metabolic function, while warm light in the evening helps cue the body for rest (Wurtman, 1975; Vetter et al., 2011). Circadian-supportive lighting systems that shift in temperature and intensity throughout the day can enhance emotional regulation and prevent fatigue or sleep disruption (Maroto-Gómez et al., 2023). 

12 

Figure 2.12: Circadian clock and its primary related functions (Biometrics 2023).  

Visual comfort is also critical. Lighting that causes glare, flicker, or harsh contrast strains the eyes and increases mental effort. Poor visual ergonomics lead to quicker cognitive depletion. Instead, effective systems support a variety of visual tasks while minimizing distraction (Osterhaus et al., 2015; Han et al., 2021). 

Finally, intuitive lighting systems reduce cognitive load. When users spend less time adjusting lighting and more time focused on their work, overall performance improves. Features such as automation, adaptive settings, and user-friendly interfaces reduce friction and enhance usability (Chen et al., 2020; Almasi et al., 2018). 

Integrating these human factors into lighting design enables more ergonomic, intuitive, and health-supportive environments. This is particularly vital in home-offices or multi-use spaces where lighting needs may shift frequently. 

2.7 Smart Lighting Systems and Market Trends 

Smart lighting systems have reshaped how users interact with light, particularly in workspaces where personalization and adaptability are highly valued (See figure 2.13). These systems combine sensors, automation, and digital control interfaces to provide lighting tailored to user routines and environmental conditions (Chen et al., 2020). 

13 

Figure 2.13: Various Desk Lighting Setups. 

Unlike static lighting, smart systems adjust brightness, color temperature, and hue dynamically throughout the day. They can be programmed to follow circadian cycles, enhancing focus in the morning and supporting relaxation in the evening (Maroto-Gómez et al., 2023). This functionality boosts cognitive performance and well-being across varied work environments. 

Smart lighting integrates seamlessly with mobile apps, voice assistants, and smart home ecosystems. These controls make it easy to access pre-set modes, automate routines, and customize preferences without requiring technical knowledge. As UX/UI design has evolved, the barrier to entry has lowered, making such systems more accessible (Almasi et al., 2018). 

Energy efficiency is another driver. Smart lighting systems often feature occupancy sensors, scheduling tools, and daylight harvesting, which contribute to both sustainability and reduced operational costs. 

Despite these benefits, many commercial smart lighting products focus more on aesthetics than function. RGB features, for instance, are often designed for visual appeal but are not integrated with behavioral or cognitive insights. There remains a gap in products that truly support focus, comfort, and psychological needs throughout the workday (Van de Werff et al., 2019). 

Market trends point to rising demand for personalization, wellness-driven environments, and seamless digital integration (See figure 2.14). These trends highlight the opportunity to create lighting systems that are not only smart but also evidence-based and user-centered (See figure 2.15).

2.8 Conclusion of Literature Review and Project Direction 

This chapter establishes that lighting significantly affects cognitive function, mood, circadian health, and performance. As workspace needs evolve, lighting must do more than illuminate—it must support the mental, emotional, and physical demands of work. 

The review highlights how adjustable lighting systems, color theory, and circadian science can be leveraged to create healthier, more productive environments. Natural light improves well-being but is often unavailable. Artificial and smart lighting systems offer alternatives, yet many current products lack meaningful adaptability or behavioral insight. 

Identified gaps include the underutilization of RGB lighting for cognitive benefit, insufficient integration of circadian-responsive features, and a lack of systems designed for varied home workspace needs. These gaps present design opportunities. 

The following chapters will explore these opportunities through user research, testing, and iterative development. Insights from this literature review will directly inform the design of a smart desk lighting system that addresses the psychological, ergonomic, and emotional demands of modern work.

Chapter III: Research Methodology  

3.1 Research Design and Approach 

This research study is structured into three key components: a literature review, empirical data collection, and a proof-of-concept system design. Each component is designed to build upon the findings of the previous phase, creating a comprehensive approach to developing a smart home lighting system that enhances productivity and cognition for students and professionals working from home. The methodological framework integrates qualitative insights (user observations, persona creation) with quantitative data (survey responses, cognitive performance testing). This dual approach ensures a balance between user experience and objective measurement. 

This study employs mixed-methods research design grounded in user-centered design and empirical testing. The goal is to understand user preferences, performance outcomes, and environmental conditions related to lighting and cognitive function in desk-based workspaces. A combination of surveys, workplace observations, and user performance testing was selected to provide a comprehensive understanding of lighting impacts on user productivity, comfort, and focus. The study is guided by color theory and ergonomic lighting principles to inform the development of a smart lighting system. 

Given the research goal to improve workspace lighting systems through color theory and personalization, a design research approach was essential. This methodology allows the researcher to iteratively gather data, test assumptions, and refine a product prototype. User-centered methods ensure relevance and effectiveness of the resulting design solution. 

3.2 IRB and Data Collection Preparation 

With human testing required for the research, a study Protocol was drawn up outlining the purpose, the methods, and the planned study documentation (See Appendix A). This protocol was approved by the University of Houston Institutional Review Board (IRB) under Study #4998.  

All participants were informed of their rights and the purpose of the study prior to participation. All participants provided informed consent and were assured of confidentiality. No personally identifiable information was collected. Responses were anonymized and securely stored. 

The study presented minimal risk to participants. Short cognitive tasks and lighting exposure were used without inducing any physical or emotional stress. Breaks were allowed, and participants could withdraw at any time. 

Participants were recruited through university networks, social media platforms, and digital flyers. Target participants were individuals who spend significant time working at a desk—including students, remote workers, and office employees. 

Inclusion criteria were simply that participants be aged 18+ and work or study at a desk at least 15 hours per week. The exclusion criteria was health based, individuals with light-sensitive medical conditions or vision impairments not corrected by glasses or contact lenses. 

3.3 Survey on Lighting Preferences, Usage, and Current Work Environment

To inform the user-centered development of the smart desk lighting system, an initial survey—referred to as the Pre-Experiment Questionnaire (see Appendix B)—was conducted to gather both quantitative and qualitative data on lighting conditions, user preferences, and desired features in workspace environments. 3.6 Research Questions and Hypotheses

4.1 Simulated Testing Environment and Prototype Setup 

The development and testing of the initial smart lighting prototype served as a foundational phase in assessing how multi-source, adjustable lighting systems influence productivity and cognitive function in home and desk-based work environments. This chapter outlines the detailed setup, methodology, and findings from the prototype testing stage. 

The user performance testing took place at the University of Houston Health-Design Lab, where a controlled workspace environment was created to simulate realistic desk-based work conditions. A standard work desk and chair were placed at the center of the space, surrounded by a multi-source lighting prototype (See figures 4.1 and 4.2). This system consisted of seven adjustable RGB lighting components: three wall-mounted LED panels, two desk-mounted LED lamps, one desk light bar, and one freestanding floor lamp. The lighting components were arranged with intentional variation in height and angle to provide comprehensive ambient, direct, and task-specific illumination. The test environment was kept minimal to prevent visual or spatial distractions. During baseline testing, only the room’s overhead fluorescent light was used, while the subsequent lighting conditions relied solely on the prototype system.

The physical prototype was built using a combination of 3D-printed structures and prefabricated LED modules. The housing and structural elements were printed in polylactic acid (PLA) and polycarbonate (PC), selected for their respective strengths in form stability and light diffusion. All light-emitting elements were RGB-addressable LED strips and panels, chosen for their ability to vary color temperature and brightness in fine increments. The prototype setup included three lighting profiles used across all testing sessions. The first profile— Baseline—consisted of only the room’s fluorescent ceiling light, offering approximately 165 lumens at a color temperature of around 3200K. 4.2 User Performance Testing and Procedure 

Participants completed six tasks designed to reflect a range of common workspace activities and cognitive demands. These tasks included both digital and analog formats, ensuring the lighting system’s effectiveness was tested under multiple modalities. The first task, Reading on Paper, required participants to read a printed paragraph and answer comprehension questions, testing clarity, shadow minimization, and visual comfort. Puzzle on Paper followed, using a word search to evaluate visual scanning, detail focus, and cognitive persistence. Reading on Computer required participants to read short digital texts and answer comprehension questions, highlighting the interaction between screen glare, contrast, and comprehension. For Typing on Computer, participants transcribed a paragraph read aloud by the researcher. This unique approach assessed memory retention, auditory processing, and keyboard visibility. Gaming on Computer involved playing Tetris at level 5 for three minutes, a task selected for its demands on decision-making, visual acuity, and hand-eye coordination. Lastly, Sketching on Paper prompted participants to draw specific objects, which tested precision, shadow clarity, and the creative interaction with lighting. 

After completing the tasks under each lighting profile (See figure 4.4), participants filled out a structured questionnaire assessing their eye comfort, perceived glare or shadowing, color and tone quality, ability to focus, and any distractions caused by the lighting. These surveys employed Likert scales as well as open-

During tasks, participants were encouraged to share thoughts aloud (think-aloud protocol), which were either recorded or transcribed in real-time. Observations by the researcher also tracked body posture, task engagement, and behavioral adjustments made in response to lighting conditions.

In addition to static presets, the Custom & Adaptive Modes allow for a personalized and responsive experience. Auto Mode dynamically adjusts lighting throughout the day based on a combination of user activity, environmental factors (such as daylight levels), and circadian cycle. Users can also save up to three personal lighting presets with customized brightness, temperature, hue, and zone settings. These presets can be labeled to reflect specific use cases—such as “Finals Week,” “Client Meeting,” or “Design Crit Day”—and reordered based on frequency or time-based behavior. The app also offers suggestions for preset usage based on historical patterns. 

Together, these lighting presets provide users with the flexibility to shape their environment to suit the demands of their daily activities—whether analytical, visual, expressive, or restorative. Each preset integrates seamlessly with the system’s control interface and scheduling tools, allowing users to transform their workspace lighting in seconds.

5.2 Final Refinements Incorporating User Testing Results 

User testing played a pivotal role in refining the direction of the final product. While early multisource setups offered comprehensive lighting coverage, participant feedback made it clear that users preferred a more cohesive, consolidated system that would reduce visual clutter and feel more integrated in both function and form. Users wanted lighting that could shift modes seamlessly while maintaining a minimal footprint and an intuitive user experience. Comments such as “some lights felt intrusive in the space” and “components felt like separate entities rather than a cohesive system” directly influenced the move toward a unified lighting solution housed within a single, adaptive product form. 

As a result, the final design— lūm —emerged as an all-in-one lighting system that retains the flexibility of multi-zone control while condensing its physical presence into a singular vertical structure. Telescoping features, articulating light elements, and integrated ambient solutions collectively respond to both functional needs and spatial constraints identified through the testing process. 

lūm is designed to transition effortlessly between multiple lighting modes while remaining compact and visually balanced. The product integrates three primary types of illumination—task lighting, direct lighting, and ambient lighting—within a single sculptural form. Each lighting element is contextually responsive, with form changes and user adjustments determining the intensity, angle, and output of the system. The final design allows users to adjust light based on cognitive state, task intensity, and environmental conditions without introducing unnecessary distraction or hardware complexity. 

The structure includes a telescoping vertical housing, a fully flexible gooseneck task light, and two distinct sources of ambient light. These features are unified within a warm, natural material palette and refined silhouette that supports both functional performance and aesthetic neutrality. A simplified interaction model allows lighting behavior to be automated or controlled via the app, supporting users who desire precision as well as those seeking set-it-and-forget-it workflows.

A telescoping light body that adjusts vertically to modulate light direction and strength. (See figure 5.9).

Task lighting is delivered through a fully adjustable gooseneck light arm, offering an extended range of motion and fine-tuned control over lighting angle, height, and direction. This highly flexible design

allows users to bend, rotate, and reposition the arm freely to suit any posture or task—from angled reading to side-lit sketching to front-facing note-taking. Once positioned, the gooseneck holds its form securely, providing a stable and targeted beam that minimizes hand shadows and maximizes focus. 

User testing highlighted a clear demand for lighting that could adapt to individual work styles and desk arrangements. In response, the task light was designed to deliver high-intensity illumination with a color temperature range of 2200K to 6500K and full brightness control from 1% to 100%, enabling it to meet both general and specialized needs. Whether users require cool, crisp light for digital work or warm, low-glare light for late-night studying, the task light’s adjustability supports long-term visual comfort and task performance. 

When not in use, the arm can be neatly folded back toward the body of the product, preserving lūm’s compact profile and visual simplicity. Its integration reinforces the system’s core promise—creating a lighting experience that is intelligent, ergonomic, and entirely user-driven. 

Direct lighting is revealed when the telescoping mechanism of lūm is extended upward. This motion unveils a larger vertical light panel housed within the core of the structure, providing strong, even illumination across the full workspace. The direct lighting element is especially suited to activities like planning, digital multitasking, and high-visibility work, where broad lighting coverage is preferred over targeted directionality. 

This lighting mode balances output and visual comfort through a subtly frosted finish that diffuses the light without dulling brightness, minimizing the harshness often associated with overhead or desk mounted LEDs. Users can activate this lighting mode independently or in conjunction with task or ambient settings. 

Ambient lighting in the final product is addressed through a dual-system approach, enhancing versatility and user comfort while supporting a variety of workspace configurations. 

The first ambient source is integrated directly into the lower section of the main body, behind a semi-translucent diffuser panel. When lūm is in its non-telescoped (resting) state, light projected behind this material is gently diffused outward, transforming what would otherwise be direct light into a warm, low-glare ambient glow. This soft illumination is ideal for use during screen breaks, wind-down periods, or low-stimulation tasks. When the product is expanded into its telescoped state, the upper direct lighting panel is exposed, delivering higher illumination while the diffuser panel below becomes inactive. When not in use as a lighting element, the diffuser panel adds a visual layer of materiality and subtle contrast to the overall aesthetic, supporting the product’s warm, sculptural identity. 

The second ambient source comes from a detached unit housing a retractable screen that reflects LED light embedded into the side of lūm’s main housing. This screen was originally conceived as an integrated retractable feature but was separated into a freestanding component due to manufacturability and mechanical constraints. The reflective screen is made of a matte, light-diffusing surface, allowing it to bounce soft ambient light back into the workspace—ideal for users who aren’t positioned near a wall or vertical surface. 

To deploy the screen, users press a push-release tab, extending the panel outward. Embedded magnets allow the tab to align and attach securely to the housing, ensuring ease of use and correct positioning every time. This feature enables users to introduce soft, reflected light for tasks like video calls, background glow, or decompression modes, regardless of workspace architecture. Together, these two

Chapter VI: Conclusion and Future Scope 

6.1 Summary of Findings 

This research set out to explore how the integration of color theory into smart lighting systems can improve productivity, cognitive performance, and user comfort within home and work environments. Through a mixed-methods approach—comprising literature review, surveys, observational research, and prototype testing—a proof-of-concept system was developed and evaluated. 

Key findings confirmed that lighting—particularly color temperature, directionality, and luminance—has measurable effects on cognitive focus and task performance. Participants in the testing phase reported greater comfort and improved performance under adjustable lighting profiles, particularly when using optimized cool white light for high-focus tasks and warmer tones for relaxation or creative work. Customizability and adaptability emerged as critical features, with users preferring systems that allow manual and automated transitions throughout the day. 

The study also highlighted the importance of visual integration and product cohesion. Early testing of the multi-component lighting setup revealed that users found the scattered lights visually distracting and functionally disconnected. This insight led to the development of a unified, compact lighting system— lūm —that consolidates task, direct, and ambient lighting into a single, adaptable unit. 

The final prototype demonstrated that flexible lighting—both in form and function—can directly support user well-being and performance. Task completion accuracy and speed improved under tailored lighting conditions, and participant feedback emphasized the positive impact of color temperature alignment with task type. 

The introduction of ambient lighting via both a soft diffusion panel and an optional reflective screen added significant value. Users noted reduced eye strain and improved mood during non-intensive tasks and screen breaks, validating research on reflected lighting’s role in cognitive comfort. The gooseneck task light also proved to be highly effective in supporting various user postures and preferences, offering a freeform approach to focus lighting. 

While the initial design direction focused on multisource modular lighting, the strongest feedback during testing was the desire for visual and physical simplification. Many users found the multi-part system excessive, especially in smaller workspaces, and emphasized the value of a product that felt “intentional” and “unified.” This led to a conceptual shift toward a monolithic, expandable design. 

Another unexpected outcome was the level of appreciation for reflective ambient lighting. Initially considered an auxiliary feature, it became one of the most positively received elements—particularly among participants without access to wall surfaces or natural bounce lighting. This validated the value of modular, optional components in smart lighting design.  

As remote work and flexible scheduling continue to reshape professional environments, the demand for home-adaptable, cognitively supportive lighting solutions will grow. This study demonstrates that lighting systems designed with user adaptability, ergonomic flexibility, and sensory comfort in mind can significantly enhance daily work routines. 

In future workspaces—whether home offices, shared studios, or institutional learning environments— lighting will likely play a hybrid role: both functionally illuminating the task at hand and emotionally 

63 

supporting well-being. Systems like lūm set a precedent for lighting that is neither static nor purely reactive but instead intelligent, responsive, and customizable to individual rhythms and needs. 

6.2 Limitations and Challenges 

Several limitations shaped the scope of this study. First, the sample size of ten user testers limited the breadth of demographic diversity, particularly across age ranges and occupational fields. Testing was also conducted in a controlled lab environment, which, while ideal for consistency, does not fully represent the wide range of lighting conditions present in real homes and workspaces. 

Technically, the prototype was constrained by hardware compatibility and software limitations. Initial plans for dynamic task-based lighting transitions had to be simplified due to connectivity issues, which required lighting profiles to remain fixed during each session. Similarly, while the detachable reflective screen was developed as a workaround for manufacturability, its separation from the main housing may affect long-term user engagement with the feature. 

Despite these limitations, the study provided strong directional insight and validation for the broader goals of adaptive, user-centered lighting. 

6.3 Future Research Opportunities 

The findings from this thesis open several exciting avenues for continued exploration and refinement. 

A natural next step is the integration of artificial intelligence to support predictive and adaptive lighting behavior. Future versions of this system could analyze user patterns, circadian cycles, environmental data, and productivity metrics to suggest or automatically adjust lighting throughout the day. AI-driven personalization could also account for nuanced user feedback—such as mood states, energy levels, or task complexity—transforming lighting from a tool into an intelligent co-pilot. 

While this study focused on individual home workspaces, the system has broader potential in shared environments such as classrooms, libraries, coworking spaces, or healthcare settings. Each of these contexts presents new design challenges related to scale, interactivity, and accessibility. Expanding the system’s physical and digital adaptability could lead to future product lines tailored to institutional needs or group-based lighting control. 

Applications in neurodivergent-friendly design also represent a promising area for future work. Features like low-stimulation ambient light, simplified interfaces, and sensory customization could support greater inclusivity across a wider user base. 

6.4 Closing Thoughts 

This thesis set out to answer a simple but powerful question: how can smart lighting be reimagined to better serve the cognitive, emotional, and functional needs of today’s evolving work environments? Through research, testing, and iterative design, the resulting product concept— lūm —offers one answer. It is a lighting system that adapts, reflects, and supports—not only illuminating the workspace but enhancing the way users engage with it. 

By integrating the principles of color theory, human factors, and user-centered design, this project demonstrates that lighting can be more than just a backdrop to productivity—it can be an active agent in shaping it. And while lūm represents a strong proof of concept, it also signals the beginning of new design possibilities where lighting becomes not only smarter, but also more human.
