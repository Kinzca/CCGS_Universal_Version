From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Computer graphics shading and rendering technique 

The ambient occlusion map (middle image) for this scene darkens only the innermost angles of corners.

In [3D computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_computer_graphics), [modeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_modeling), and [animation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_animation), **ambient occlusion** is a [shading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shading) and [rendering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rendering_(computer_graphics)) technique used to calculate how exposed each point in a scene is to [ambient lighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shading#Ambient_lighting). For example, the interior of a tube is typically more occluded (and hence darker) than the exposed outer surfaces, and becomes darker the deeper inside the tube one goes.

Ambient occlusion can be seen as an accessibility value that is calculated for each surface point. [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_occlusion#cite_note-1) In scenes with open sky this is done by estimating the amount of visible sky for each point, while in indoor environments only objects within a certain radius are taken into account and the walls are assumed to be the origin of the ambient light. The result is a [diffuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_reflection), non-directional shading effect that casts no clear shadows, but that darkens enclosed and sheltered areas and can affect the rendered image's overall tone. It is often used as a [post-processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_editing) effect.

Unlike local methods such as [Phong shading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phong_shading), ambient occlusion is a global method, meaning that the illumination at each point is a function of other geometry in the scene. However, it is a very crude approximation to full [global illumination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_illumination). The appearance achieved by ambient occlusion alone is similar to the way an object might appear on an [overcast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overcast) day.

The first method that allowed simulating ambient occlusion in real time was developed by the research and development department of [Crytek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crytek) ( [CryEngine 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CryEngine)). [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_occlusion#cite_note-2) With the release of hardware capable of real time ray tracing ( [GeForce 20 series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GeForce_20_series)) by [Nvidia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nvidia) in 2018, [ray traced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_tracing_(graphics)) ambient occlusion (RTAO) became possible in games and other real time applications. [[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_occlusion#cite_note-3) This feature was added to the [Unreal Engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreal_Engine) with version 4.22. [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_occlusion#cite_note-4)

## Implementation

[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ambient_occlusion&action=edit&section=1)]  

3D animation of ambient occlusion enabled on the animation to the right

In the absence of hardware-assisted [ray traced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_tracing_(graphics)) ambient occlusion, [real-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-time_computer_graphics) applications such as computer games can use [screen space ambient occlusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screen_space_ambient_occlusion) (SSAO) techniques such as [horizon-based ambient occlusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon-based_ambient_occlusion) including HBAO and [ground-truth ambient occlusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ground-truth_ambient_occlusion&action=edit&redlink=1) (GTAO) as a faster approximation of true ambient occlusion, using [per-pixel depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z-buffering), rather than scene geometry, to form an ambient occlusion [map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_array).

Ambient occlusion is related to accessibility shading, which determines appearance based on how easy it is for a surface to be touched by various elements (e.g., dirt, light, etc.). It has been popularized in production animation due to its relative simplicity and efficiency.

The ambient occlusion shading model offers a better perception of the 3D shape of the displayed objects. This was shown in a paper where the authors report the results of perceptual experiments showing that depth discrimination under diffuse uniform sky lighting is superior to that predicted by a direct lighting model. [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_occlusion#cite_note-5)
